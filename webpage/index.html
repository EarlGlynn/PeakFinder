SUMMARY

Background: In eukaryotic cells, cohesin holds sister chromatids together until they separate into daughter cells during mitosis.

Methodology/Principle Findings: We have used chromatin immunoprecipitation coupled with microarray analysis (ChIP chip) to produce a genome-wide description of cohesin binding to meiotic and mitotic chromosomes of S. cerevisiae. The identification of cohesin binding peaks in ChIP chip data was facilitated by a novel automated algorithm, PeakFinder.

Conclusions/Significance: Cohesin sites are highly conserved in meiosis and mitosis, suggesting that chromosomes share a common underlying structure during different developmental programs. A negative correlation between cohesin sites and meiotic recombination sites suggests meiotic exchange is spatially regulated to promote exchange between homologs rather than sister chromatids. On chromosome arms cohesin associated regions occur with a semi-periodic spacing of 11 kb that correlate with AT content. The number of sites correlates with chromosome size. Cohesin binding to neighboring sites is not cooperative. Cohesin sites are strongly biased towards regions between convergent transcription units. Transcript elongation into a cohesin binding site inhibits cohesin binding. Thus transcription and cohesion are apparently incompatible processes. This genome-wide view of mitotic and meiotic cohesin binding provides an important framework for the exploration of cohesins and cohesion in other genomes.
